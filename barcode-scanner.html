Everything You Need to Know About Our Online Barcode Scanner

This guide walks through what a barcode is, how our decoder works, which formats you can expect to see in the wild, and how professionals use a browser barcode scanner to move faster.

1. What Is a Barcode Scanner?

A barcode scanner is a piece of software or hardware that translates the visual pattern of bars and spaces (or dots, in 2D codes) into the numeric or alphanumeric value they encode. Our online barcode scanner replaces the traditional laser gun with a camera and a decoder that runs directly in your browser — the same math, none of the hardware.

Hardware vs. software scanners

Handheld scanners are purpose-built and blindingly fast, but they lock you into one form factor. A modern browser barcode scanner like ours turns any phone, tablet or laptop into an equally capable device, with the added benefits of instant updates and format coverage that traditional scanners rarely match.

  • Universal reach — any device with a camera becomes a scanner.
  • Always current — new symbologies are shipped as browser updates.
  • Zero maintenance — no drivers, firmware, or dead batteries.

3. 1D vs. 2D Barcodes Explained

Not every barcode is created equal. The difference between a linear (1D) code and a matrix (2D) code determines both how much data it can hold and how it should be scanned.

Linear (1D) barcodes

These are the vertical-stripe codes you see on nearly every retail product. They typically store 8–20 digits and require a scan line to cross every bar. EAN-13, UPC-A and Code 128 are the most common examples.

Matrix (2D) barcodes

QR, Data Matrix, PDF417 and Aztec pack data in two dimensions, storing hundreds to thousands of characters and remaining readable even if partially damaged thanks to Reed–Solomon error correction.

  • 1D best for — retail SKUs, shelf labels, ID tags.
  • 2D best for — URLs, boarding passes, vCards, small-part marking.

4. Using the Camera Scanner

The camera mode of our barcode scanner camera flow is designed to feel exactly like a native app. On mobile it selects the rear camera automatically, applies continuous autofocus, and streams frames to the decoder at up to 60 fps.

Tips for reliable camera scans

  • Hold the code roughly parallel to the sensor — up to 45° tilt is tolerated.
  • Fill about a third of the frame with the barcode for the fastest lock.
  • Avoid direct glare — a slight angle beats a reflective one.
  • On desktops, an external webcam of at least 1080p makes a real difference.

10. Tips to Improve Recognition Accuracy

Even a great barcode decoder can be tripped up by a poor capture. If a scan won't lock, try these fixes in order:

  • Move closer — the barcode should fill roughly a third of the frame.
  • Steady the phone against a surface to remove motion blur.
  • Turn on the LED torch if the label is in shadow.
  • Wipe the camera lens — smudges silently ruin small 2D codes.
  • For creased or curved labels, flatten first and try again.
